About Conway’s Game of Life

Conway’s Game of Life is a mesmerizing cellular automaton invented by mathematician John Horton Conway in 1970. It’s a zero-player game: set the starting state, and watch as patterns evolve-no further input needed!

How does it work?

  • Each cell is either alive or dead on a grid.
  • Each cell interacts with its eight neighbors (adjacent horizontally, vertically, or diagonally).
  • At each step, these rules apply:
    • Any live cell with <2 live neighbors dies (underpopulation).
    • Any live cell with 2 or 3 live neighbors lives on.
    • Any live cell with >3 live neighbors dies (overpopulation).
    • Any dead cell with exactly 3 live neighbors becomes alive (reproduction).

Why is it fascinating?

From simple beginnings, the Game of Life creates complex, beautiful patterns-oscillators, gliders, spaceships, even Turing machines! It’s a classic demonstration of emergence: how simple rules can lead to surprising complexity.
